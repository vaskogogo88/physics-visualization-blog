Exploring Non-linear Dynamics: From Simple Harmonics to Deterministic Chaos

Introduction

This project presents an interactive study of Pohl's torsion pendulum, utilizing a custom-built data pipeline to explore dynamic behaviors ranging from simple harmonic motion to chaotic oscillations. By analyzing the system across four distinct regimes, specifically undamped, damped, damped-driven, and chaotic, we demonstrate how incremental changes in parameters such as friction, external driving forces, and potential well configurations fundamentally alter the system's evolution.

Using Python and video tracking software, I extracted high-resolution angular displacement, velocity, and acceleration data to construct the interactive 3D phase space trajectories featured below. The transition to a double-well potential successfully induced non-linear behavior, which can be clearly seen from the resulting phase space and time series trajectories.

Results

Harmonic Motion

In the first part of the experiment, we investigated the behavior of Pohl's torsion pendulum under simple harmonic motion, where the only acting force was the restoring torque exerted by the spring. The rotating disk of the pendulum was manually displaced from its equilibrium position and released from rest, allowing it to oscillate freely from different initial angular displacements \(\phi_0\) without any initial velocity \(\omega_0\).

Although no external driving forces were applied, as shown in the time series plot \(\phi = f(t)\), a slight exponential decay is observed due to frictional forces acting within the system. The presence of friction alters the trajectory shown in the Harmonic Phase Space diagram below. Under ideal, undamped conditions, this path would follow a perfectly closed orbit. Finally, from the 3D state space plot, we can see that as time progresses, both the angular displacement \(\phi\) and the angular velocity \(\omega\) approach zero.

Damped Motion

In the second part of the experiment, we examine the behavior of Pohl's torsion pendulum when subjected to a damping force arising from Eddy currents induced by the magnetic coils. The total damping results from the superposition of mechanical friction and these electromagnetic forces. As a result, a more pronounced decay in the amplitude of oscillation is observed compared to the simple harmonic case.

As observed from the damped time series plot, the system converges to equilibrium significantly faster while still maintaining its oscillatory nature. This demonstrates the transition toward a higher damping ratio \(\delta\), where energy is dissipated more rapidly through the disk's interaction with the magnetic field.

Driven Motion

In this section, we attempted to counteract damping losses by introducing an external periodic driving force. While five different frequencies were tested, only one is featured here to demonstrate the system reaching steady-state. This condition occurs once initial transients have decayed, resulting in a stable synchronization between the driving force and the pendulum's response. The phase space for this regime reveals a limit cycle, closely resembling the behavior of an ideal oscillator.

Chaotic Motion

Observing chaotic behavior in Pohl's torsion pendulum requires introducing a double-well potential, which creates the necessary nonlinear restoring forces. This was achieved by attaching two masses symmetrically to the pendulum. We first examined the pendulum's motion without applying any external driving force, allowing it to oscillate from two slightly different initial angular displacements \(\phi_{01}\) and \(\phi_{02}\) to test for sensitive dependence on initial conditions.

The sensitivity to initial conditions can be seen from the Chaotic Motion Experiment 1 and Experiment 2 time series plots. The system reaches equilibrium at different angular displacements \(\phi\), depending on the initial conditions. This behavior arises from the presence of the double potential well, which allows the pendulum to settle into different local minima based on its starting position.

Conclusion

In this experiment, we investigated the dynamic behavior of Pohl's torsion pendulum under different physical conditions. Beginning with simple harmonic motion, we confirmed the theoretical predictions for oscillations with and without damping. By introducing an external periodic torque, we were able to compensate for energy loss and study the system's transition to steady-state oscillations.

The most intriguing results emerged when the pendulum was modified to introduce a double potential well, enabling the observation of nonlinear and chaotic dynamics. We demonstrated that small changes in initial conditions could lead to significantly different outcomes.

Overall, the study highlights the rich and diverse behaviors present in even a simple mechanical system and provides insight into the onset of chaos in deterministic physical systems. The experimental results align well with theoretical expectations and reinforce key principles of classical and nonlinear dynamics.
